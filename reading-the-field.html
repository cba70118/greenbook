What this is

A plain-language window into how a statistical model evaluates a golf tournament — built so a complete beginner can read it. Use the tabs to move between the model’s rankings, the simulated probabilities, and short lessons on how golf betting markets work.

The one idea to take away

Betting is not about picking who’s good. It’s about price versus reality. Everyone knows Scottie Scheffler is the best player. That’s already baked into his price. The only question that matters is whether a player’s true chance is higher or lower than what the odds imply. This whole guide is about estimating that true chance — and seeing where it disagrees with the crowd.

How the model thinks (in three steps)

1 · Rank the field, blind
No odds, no narratives.

Rate every player on the skills this course demands — here, approach play above all — using strokes-gained data. The result is the Power Rankings tab.

2 · Simulate the tournament

Run the event tens of thousands of times to turn skill estimates into real probabilities: each player’s chance to win, finish top-5, top-10, top-20, or make the cut. The Win Probabilities tab.

3 · Compare to the crowd

Hold the model’s view up against the consensus. Where they disagree is the interesting part — players the crowd may over- or under-rate. The Model vs Consensus tab.

Order matters: the ranking is formed before looking at prices, so the price can’t bias the read. That’s the whole discipline — form an independent opinion first, then compare.

Why Shinnecock rewards what it rewards

Every course favors a different kind of player. Knowing which kind is the entire edge in pre-tournament analysis. Here’s Shinnecock in plain English.

The one-sentence version

Shinnecock is a complete-game test where approach play is king — there is no single skill a player can hide behind, and the irons matter most of all.

What the course does to players

  • Tiny effective targets. The greens are large but firm and severely contoured, and the U.S. Open hides the pins on slopes. So even good approaches can be rejected — elite iron play (approach) is the #1 separator.
  • Brutal rough. Four-inch fescue means a missed fairway is a real penalty. You don’t have to be the longest hitter, but you can’t spray it.
  • The hardest short game on tour. Miss a green and you face run-offs and deep bunkers. Scrambling and sand saves are punishing — a weak short game gets exposed.
  • Treacherous putting. Big, sloping poa/bent greens at U.S. Open speed. Three-putts lurk.
  • Nothing plays easy. Par 3s, 4s, and 5s all grade “difficult.” Patience beats firepower.

So who fits?

Thrives

Precise iron players with a complete bag — good approach, solid short game, and the temperament to grind out pars. Accuracy over raw distance.

Exposed

One-dimensional players — bombers who spray it, or specialists carried by a hot putter. The course will find the hole in their game.

This is why, in the heat map, a green Approach cell is worth more than a green Driving cell this week. A different course (a short, soft, bomber’s track) would flip that.

Betting 101 — how golf markets work

Golf isn’t a two-team game — you’re picking from 156 players, so the markets look different. Here are the ones you’ll see, and how to read a price.

The market types

Outright / Win
Bet on who wins the tournament. Big payouts, but even the favorite is usually only ~10–12% — you’ll lose this bet most weeks. That’s normal.
Finishing position (Top 5 / 10 / 20)
Bet a player finishes inside that bucket. Lower odds, hits far more often — the gentler way to back a player.
Each-Way (E/W)
Two bets in one: half your stake on the win, half on a place (a top-5, top-8, or top-10 finish, depending on the book’s terms). If your player places but doesn’t win, the place half pays at a fraction of the win odds. See the box below.
First-Round Leader (FRL)
Who leads after Thursday. A pure-variance lottery — huge odds, tiny edges.
Matchups / 3-balls
Head-to-head: which of two (or three) players shoots lower. No need to beat the whole field — just the opponent.

Reading “Each-Way 1/4, top-5”

Say a player is 40/1 each-way, 1/4 odds, top-5, and you stake 1 unit each way (2 units total):

  • He wins: the win half pays 40/1 and the place half pays (because winning is also a top-5).
  • He finishes 2nd–5th: the win half loses, but the place half pays 1/4 of 40/1 = 10/1.
  • He finishes 6th or worse: both halves lose.

“1/4” is the place fraction; “top-5” is how many spots pay. Different books offer 1/4 top-5, 1/5 top-8, 1/5 top-10 — wider nets pay smaller dividends.

Reading the odds

American odds tell you the payout and the implied probability:

  • +220 → risk $100 to win $220. Implied chance ≈ 31% (formula: 100 / (220 + 100)).
  • −150 → risk $150 to win $100. Implied chance ≈ 60% (150 / (150 + 100)).
The vig (the house’s cut). Add up the implied chances of every player and you get well over 100% — that overage is the bookmaker’s margin. It’s why beating the market is hard, and why you compare a player’s odds against different books to find the best price.

Dead-heat rules (the rule that surprises beginners)

If players tie for the last paying spot — say five players tie for a top-5 place — the payout is divided. You don’t get the full place return; you get a fraction. It’s a real, common haircut on finishing-position and each-way bets, not a technicality.

Bankroll & discipline

The math of staying in the game. None of this is advice to bet — it’s the framework serious bettors use to survive variance, which is the part beginners always underestimate.

Think in units, not dollars

A “unit” is one fixed slice of your total bankroll — commonly 1–2%. If your bankroll is $1,000, a unit is ~$10–20. You size every bet in units, so a bad week can’t wipe you out and your stakes scale with your bankroll automatically.

Why bet sizes should vary — the Kelly idea

The bigger your edge (your estimated chance vs. the price), the more you stake — but only proportionally. Most disciplined bettors use a fraction of the theoretical optimum (e.g. “quarter-Kelly”) because our probability estimates are themselves uncertain. Smaller fractions trade a little growth for a lot less risk of ruin.

The four rules that keep beginners solvent

  • Cap your weekly exposure. Decide the most you’ll risk in a week before you look at any cards, and don’t exceed it.
  • A positive-edge bet still loses most of the time. A 35%-to-place bet misses 65% of the time. Losing is not evidence the bet was wrong.
  • Never chase. Raising stakes to “win it back” is how bankrolls die. The math doesn’t care that you’re down.
  • Judge decisions, not results. Over a season, good process and bad luck both happen — track whether you’re getting good prices, not just whether bets won.
Variance, concretely. If you make ten 25%-chance bets, the likeliest single outcome is that 2–3 hit — but runs of zero-from-eight happen routinely. A model can be right and still endure long droughts. Plan for the drought, or it will end you before the edge pays off.

How many strokes better or worse than the field average a player is in a category. The modern way to measure golf skill.
SG: OTT / APP / ARG / PUTT
Off-the-tee (driving), Approach (iron play), Around-the-green (short game/chipping), and Putting. The four pieces of a player’s game.
Course fit
How well a player’s strengths match what a specific course rewards. A great player can still be a poor fit.
Implied probability
The chance baked into a price. +200 implies ~33%.
Edge
Your estimated probability minus the implied probability. Positive edge = the bet is “worth more” than its price, in theory.
EV (Expected Value)
The average outcome of a bet if you could repeat it forever. Positive EV is the goal; it does not mean any single bet wins.
Each-Way (E/W)
A combined win + place bet. See Betting 101.
Place / finishing-position
A bet on a top-N finish rather than the win.
FRL
First-Round Leader.
Dead heat
A tie for a paying position; the payout is split.
Vig / juice
The bookmaker’s built-in margin.
CLV (Closing Line Value)
Whether you got a better price than the final, closing odds. A key long-run measure of whether you’re beating the market.
Unit
One fixed slice of bankroll used to size bets consistently.
Kelly
A formula for sizing bets in proportion to edge; most use a fraction of it.
Variance
The natural swing of results around the true probability. High in golf.
Form ratio
Recent scoring relative to a player’s baseline. Above 1.00 = trending up.
About this guide. An educational, statistics-only reference for the 2026 U.S. Open at Shinnecock Hills. Rankings and the skill map come from an independent course-fit model built on strokes-gained data; the probabilities come from a tournament simulation; the “consensus” comparison uses DataGolf’s public model. Nothing here is betting advice, a tip, or a recommendation to wager. Probabilities are estimates with real uncertainty. 126 players rated; amateurs and qualifiers without ratings are omitted. If you choose to bet, do so legally and within your means.
